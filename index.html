Keränen’s g₈₅ morphism (1992): The first known abelian square-free endomorphism over 4 letters. Each of the four letters maps to a word of length 85. Applying the morphism iteratively starting from “a” produces an infinite abelian square-free word. The key property is that g(b), g(c), and g(d) are cyclic permutations of g(a) — this guarantees their Parikh vectors are equal, preventing abelian squares across block boundaries.
Turtle Walk: Each letter maps to a direction — a→Right, b→Up, c→Left, d→Down. abelian square-free words have a characteristic non-repeating drift pattern in this walk. Notice the walk never returns to the same region in the same way: that visual non-repetition mirrors the algebraic non-repetition in the sequence.

Sonification (Music)

Listen to the structure of the g₈₅ word. Slicing into chunks of 85 corresponds to exactly one morphism image per track. Because g(b), g(c), g(d) are cyclic permutations of g(a), each track shares the same set of pitches — only their order changes.

What to listen for: Each chunk of 85 letters is one “block” of the morphism. Because the images of a, b, c, d are cyclic permutations of each other, all chunks contain exactly the same pitch classes (C, E, G, A — a C major 6th chord). The rhythm shifts block by block, but no rhythmic pattern ever repeats in the same way — that is the sonic signature of abelian square-freeness. Try chunk sizes of 1, 85, or 170 for different effects.

The Road to 4 Letters

How many letters does it take to build an infinite abelian square-free word? This question took over three decades to answer — click the events to explore.

Key milestones: Erdős posed the problem in 1961. Pleasants (1970) showed 5 letters suffice. Dekking (1979) proved 3 letters are impossible. The gap between 3 and 5 was finally closed by Keränen (1992) showing 4 letters are enough — settling the problem completely. Open questions remain about the exact number and structure of such words and morphisms.

Unfavorable Factors

Certain short words (“unfavorable factors”) cannot be extended far in either direction without creating an abelian square. Enter a word or pick an example, then see how far left and right the word can grow before it gets stuck. (Concept from Keränen’s 2010 Mathematica Journal article.)

Definition — Unfavorable Factor: An abelian square-free word w is an unfavorable factor if there exists a finite bound N such that every abelian square-free word containing w as a subword has length at most N. Such words act as “dead ends” in the search tree: no matter how you try to grow them, you will always encounter an abelian square within a bounded number of steps. Keränen used this concept in his 2010 paper to analyze the structure of the infinite g₈₅ word and show which local patterns are unavoidable.

Morphism Microscope

Two abelian square-free morphisms by Veikko Keränen. Both map each of the four letters to a longer word such that the image of b, c, d are cyclic permutations of the image of a. Iterating either morphism from the seed word “a” produces an infinite abelian square-free word.

Why are there multiple morphisms? Keränen’s g₈₅ (1992) was the first construction and remains the canonical one — length 85 per image is surprisingly long. Later work (g₉₈, 2009) produced morphisms with different image lengths and combinatorial properties, demonstrating that the language of abelian square-free words over 4 letters is rich enough to support multiple distinct morphic structures. The existence of many such morphisms is itself an open research area: how many fundamentally different abelian square-free morphisms are there?

Morphism Laboratory

Design your own uniform morphism over {a,b,c,d}. The program will evaluate its Parikh vectors, check for cyclic permutations, and iteratively search for abelian squares.

What makes a morphism abelian square-free? Three necessary (but not sufficient) conditions: (1) Uniform — all four images must have the same length. (2) Cyclic Parikh — the Parikh vectors of g(a), g(b), g(c), g(d) must be cyclic permutations of one another (same letter counts, different order). (3) Non-trivial images — each image must itself be abelian square-free. Even satisfying all three, finding a morphism whose infinite iteration is abelian square-free requires additional structural constraints — Keränen’s achievement was to find one that works.
