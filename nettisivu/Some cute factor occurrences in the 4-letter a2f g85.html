Here we look at the image words g85(a), g85(b), g85(c) and g85(d).

We try to cover the whole string with colors. In the end, covering can be done almost entirely with four different colors (differences in tone allowed), but not quite. There remains a small white gap ‘dc’ that may function like punctuation or a delimiter.

A bit different coloring of the above:

As noted above, the white gap ‘dc’ and similar ones in the rows below it cannot be covered with these colors. Indeed,

appear only in those places, and this is also true for the generated omega-word obtained by iteration of g85.
The mirror images of those words of length four do not appear anywhere else.

One occurrence of c (and the letters d, a, b below it) is in bold. That occurrence can be deleted in some extreme cases, as Arturo Carpi showed in his paper in Discrete Applied Mathematics in 1998.

Consider factors dabacabadbabcbdb, abcbdbcbacbcdcac, bcdcacdcbdcdadbd, cabadbabcbdbcbacbcdcacbabd of length 16, 16, 16, 26 and their cyclic permutations.
In g85(a) below, note that abcbdbcbacbcdcac = cyclicPermutation( dabacabadbabcbdb ) and  bcdcacdcbdcdadbd = cyclicPermutation( abcbdbcbacbcdcac ).

Another view. Once again, note the cyclic permutation: dabacabadbabcbdb → abcbdbcbacbcdcac → bcdcacdcbdcdadbd → cdadbdadcadabaca.

A remarkable thing about all this is that the cyclic permutations of long factors also appear in the same image word, that is, they are appearing in unexpected places!
The original search algorithm had nothing to do with their appearance. There is apparently a logical reason for the phenomenon that we do not yet understand.
